CHAPTER SUMMARY
Real estate law is based on statutory law and common law. Statutory law includes the U.S.
Constitution and state constitutions, laws enacted by Congress and the state legislature,
departments of the federal and state bureaucracies, and ordinances adopted by local gov-
emments. Common law is the basis for the legal system in all states, except Louisiana.
The real estate industry is divided into four main categories brokerages, property
development, property management, and appraising. Brokerages, managed by a desig-
nated broker, provide for the completion of real estate transactions between buyers
and sellers. Other licensed brokers, known as associate brokers, and licensed salesper-
sons have the same privileges and responsibilities. These individuals should have a writ-
ten employment agreement indicating their status as either employees or independent
contractors. Brokerages tend to prefer independent contractors, because brokerages do
not have to provide benefits or pay Social Security and unemployment taxes for inde-
pendent contractors. However, the designated broker is still responsible for all actions
of licensed salespeople and associate brokers.
Compensation to the broker is established through negotiations between the seller
and the broker. The amount of compensation is set forth in the employment agreement
and is often a percentage of the sales price. Under the Sherman Anti-Trust Act, price
fixing is illegal. A real estate salesperson generally receives compensation in one of
two ways. If the brokerage is a commission-sharing brokerage, then a percentage of
the commission amount collected by the brokerage is paid to the salesperson. Ifthe bro-
kerage is a 100 percent brokerage, the salesperson receives all of the commission col-
lected by the brokerage, but the salesperson must pay a monthly fee to the brokerage.
The commission paid to a real estate salesperson can be paid only by his or her broker.
Arizona statutes define the actions that require a real estate salesperson or broker
license. These requirements and exemptions should be studied in full in the chapter
or through the Arizona Department of Real Estate's Website: http:/www.re.state.az.us.
The three types of general real estate licenses are real estate broker, real estate sales-
person, and real estate entity. In order to receive a broker license, the applicant must be a
U.S. citizen or resident alien, be at least 18 years old, and have had at least three ofhis or
her past five years' experience as a licensed salesperson. In addition, the applicant must
have taken 90 classroom hours and passed the school and the state exam. The require-
ments for a real estate salesperson license are nearly identical; however, applicants for a
real estate salesperson license are not required to have previous real estate experience. A
brokerage may be licensed as a corporation, limited liability company, or partnership. If
the brokerage is a business entity, then the brokerage must have a designated broker. The
designated broker must be either an officer ofthe corporation, a manager or a member of
the limited liability company, or a partner of the partnership.
The real estate examination consists of 140 multiple-choice questions (80 general
and 60 Arizona-specific). Students must receive at least a 75 percent grade on both
the general and state portions of the exam to pass. Once an applicant has passed the
state examination, he or she has 1 year to activate the license.
Prior to activating a salesperson license, the applicant must attend a 6-hour Contract
Writing course. Before activating an original broker license, a broker must attend a 3-hour
Broker Management Clinic. Arizona requires real estate licensees and brokers to renew
their licenses every 2 years. License renewal is accomplished through completion of 24
hours of continuing education. A license will expire if it is not renewed. A licensee has
1 year to reinstate an expired license by paying fees and taking the required renewal
classes. During that year, the licensee may not function as a real estate licensee.
