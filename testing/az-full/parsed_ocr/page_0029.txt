Chapter 1 . Real Estate Law and Real Estate Business
Real Estate Broker License
Applications for a real estate broker license must be in writing and signed by the appli-
cant. Applicants must be at least 18 years old and must be U.S. citizens or resident ali-
ens. The application must tell whether the applicant has ever been charged with or
convicted ofa criminal offense. The application must also disclose whether the applicant
has ever been refused a license or had a license revoked or suspended.
In order to receive a broker license, the applicant must have spent at least three of
his or her past 5 years' experience as a licensed salesperson. In addition, the applicant
must have taken 90 classroom hours (cannot be distance learning courses) and must
have passed the school and the state exams.
Applicants pay an examination fee and a license fee. Applicants must also be finger-
printed prior to receiving their licenses. Successful applicants will be licensed for 2
years. After the initial 2-year period, the license is renewed for a 4-year period.
Real Estate Salesperson License
The requirements for a real estate salesperson license are nearly identical to the require-
ments for the broker license. However, applicants for a real estate salesperson license are
not required to have previous real estate experience. Applicants for a salesperson license
also pay an examination fee and a license fee. Salesperson applicants are also initially
licensed for 2 years. After the initial period, the license is renewed for a 4-year period.
Information about fee amounts can be found at the Arizona Department of Real
Estate website: http:/www.re.state.az.us.
Real Estate Entity License
A broker may be licensed as a sole proprietorship. For this type oft brokerage, a separate
business entity is not required, and the self-employed broker is the designated broker.
Alternatively, a brokerage may be licensed as a corporation, limited liability com-
pany, or partnership. If the brokerage is a business entity, then the brokerage must
have a designated broker who is one of the following:
1. An officer of the corporation
2. A manager or member of the limited liability company
3. A partner of the partnership
The designated broker is also permitted to be a business entity, but such a business entity
can be only a professional corporation or a professional limited liability company. The
business entity may, in turn, be the designated broker for a brokerage that is a business
entity (that is, corporation, LLC, or partnership).
The designated broker, whether an individual or a business entity, may act as an
employing broker and may engage the services of separately licensed salespersons
and associate brokers.
Salespersons and associate brokers may be licensed in their individual capacity (that
is, sole proprietorship) or may be licensed as professional corporations or professional
limited liability companies. Salespersons and associate brokers may be licensed only
as these entities; they cannot be licensed as anything else (that is, LLC, corporation,
and sO on).
